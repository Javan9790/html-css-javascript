Early Life

Jomo Kenyatta was born as Kamau wa Muigai around 1897 in Gatundu, Kiambu District, in British East Africa (now Kenya). He belonged to the Kikuyu community. After the death of his parents, he was raised by relatives according to Kikuyu traditions. He later attended a mission school where he was baptized as Johnstone Kamau. His early education exposed him to Western culture and Christianity

Education and Time Abroad

In the 1920s, Kenyatta became involved in African political activism. In 1929, he traveled to Britain to present Kikuyu land grievances to the colonial government. He studied in London and later at the London School of Economics under the famous anthropologist Bronislaw Malinowski. During this period, he wrote 'Facing Mount Kenya' (1938), a book describing Kikuyu culture and traditions. 3. Political Activism and Mau Mau Period Kenyatta returned to Kenya in 1946 and became president of the Kenya African Union (KAU). He strongly advocated for land reforms and African self-governance. In 1952, during the Mau Mau uprising, he was arrested by British colonial authorities and accused of leading the rebellion. He was tried and imprisoned in Kapenguria for several years despite limited evidence linking him directly to violence.

Path to Independence

Kenyatta was released in 1961 after growing international pressure. He became leader of the Kenya African National Union (KANU). Under his leadership, Kenya gained independence from Britain on December 12, 1963. He became Kenya’s first Prime Minister and later its first President when Kenya became a republic in 1964.

Presidency (1964–1978)

As President, Kenyatta promoted national unity under the slogan 'Harambee' (pulling together). He focused on economic development, agriculture, education expansion, and infrastructure. His government maintained close ties with Western nations during the Cold War. However, his presidency also faced criticism over land distribution inequalities and political centralization.

Death and Legacy

Mzee Jomo Kenyatta died on August 22, 1978, in Mombasa, Kenya. He is remembered as the 'Father of the Nation' for leading Kenya to independence. His image appears on Kenyan currency, and major institutions and infrastructure bear his name, including Jomo Kenyatta International Airport and the University of Nairobi's Kenyatta Conference Centre nearby. His legacy remains significant in Kenya’s political and historical development
